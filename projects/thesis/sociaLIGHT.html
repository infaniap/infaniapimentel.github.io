SociaLIGHT

A wearable system that makes conversation intent visible, so starting a conversation requires less effort than avoiding one.

Wearable Behavioral Design ESP32 NFC Human Factors
Overview

Starting conversations with strangers is often avoided not because people do not want to connect, but because the effort of starting feels higher than the effort of staying silent. Phones make that worse by pulling attention inward at the exact moment outward presence matters.

SociaLIGHT is a wearable system designed to lower that barrier. Users select a conversation prompt mapped to a color state, then transfer it to the wearable with a single NFC tap. The device displays that state on a quadrant layout others can read at a glance.

Built as a Human Factors thesis project, the system was prototyped, deployed, and evaluated in a live social study using about 30 devices. The clearest finding was that visibility mattered more than perfect interpretation.

How It Works

A participant selects a physical prompt card. That selection maps to a color state and is transferred to the wearable through a single NFC tap. The display updates immediately, with no phone, pairing, or app flow involved.

Others read the signal at a glance and use it as an opening to approach. The device does not mediate the conversation, it reduces the friction of starting one. The quadrant layout supports multiple states without making the interaction harder to use.

Input (prompt card) → NFC transfer → Wearable display → Social interpretation.

My Role

I owned the project end to end, from concept through deployment. I designed the system, prototyped and assembled about 30 wearable devices, wrote the embedded firmware, designed the study, obtained IRB approval, recruited participants, facilitated sessions, and analyzed the results. Pilot behavior and hardware issues directly shaped later design and firmware decisions.

Takeaway

People used the device to start conversations even when they were not fully sure what a color meant. The signal did not need to be perfectly legible to be useful, it needed to be visible enough to invite approach.

The main gap was learnability. A next version would focus on making the system easier to interpret without adding setup friction, then testing whether the behavior holds outside a structured study setting.
